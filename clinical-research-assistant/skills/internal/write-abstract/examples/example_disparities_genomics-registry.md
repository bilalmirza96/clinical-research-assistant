# Reference example — Registry + genomics disparities abstract (Bilal's preferred style)

> Use as template for **multi-database / molecular registry abstracts** (SEER / NCDB / AACR Project GENIE), rigor-heavy, mediation-driven. Source: esophageal cancer disparities study, 2026-06-15. Note the `/ [xx%]` placeholders — these mark where numerator/denominator (n/N) are to be inserted; Bilal keeps n (%) with explicit counts.

**Objective:** Multimodal therapy has improved esophageal cancer survival, but whether these gains have been shared equitably by race is uncertain. We assessed whether the Black-White survival gap narrowed in the immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) era and, if not, whether residual disparity reflects tumor biology or inequitable access to multimodal care.

**Methods:** Matched retrospective cohort study across three platforms: SEER (n=32,820), the National Cancer Database (NCDB; n=114,633), and a multi-platform genomic dataset (133 self-reported Non-Hispanic Black [NHB] and 3,211 Non-Hispanic White [NHW] patients; AACR Project GENIE). Multivariable models adjusted for age, sex, diagnosis era, histology, stage, anatomic site, comorbidity, facility type, insurance, and area-level socioeconomic measures. Primary endpoints were verified by 1:1 propensity-score matching and inverse-probability weighting. Pre-specified sensitivity analyses included multiple imputation, Bonferroni correction, counterfactual decomposition, and E-value analysis.

**Results:** Cancer-specific survival across the ICI era improved for White patients (within-race Era 3 [2018-2022] vs Era 1 [2010-2014], SEER hazard ratio [HR], 0.87; 95% CI, 0.83-0.91; P<.001) but not for Black patients (HR, 0.92; 95% CI, 0.82-1.03; P=.16); overall survival showed a similar asymmetry (within-race Era 3 vs Era 1 HR, 0.78 [Black] vs 0.68 [White]). Over 2010-2023, Black patients had worse cancer-specific (adjusted HR, 1.29; 95% CI, 1.23-1.36; propensity-matched, 1.25) and overall (adjusted HR, 1.14; 95% CI, 1.11-1.17) survival. Among Stage I-III patients, Black patients underwent cancer-directed surgery at about half the White rate (/ [22.3%] vs / [48.0%]; adjusted odds ratio [OR], 0.50), robust within every stage and histology and across socioeconomic adjustment, facility clustering, SEER replication, and matching (OR range, 0.48-0.61; E-value, 3.41). The gap localized to the recommendation step: surgery was recorded as not recommended substantially more often for Black patients at every stage, whereas documented refusal was rare in both groups. In causal mediation analysis, differential surgery receipt accounted for about half of the survival disparity (overall survival, 50%; 95% CI, 36-65%; cancer-specific, 47%; 95% CI, 23-71%). Chemotherapy and radiation receipt were equitable. In Stage IV disease, Black patients were less likely to receive ICI (/ [10.7%] vs / [19.5%]; adjusted OR, 0.73) despite higher tumor mutational burden (/ [23.3%] vs / [10.4%]; OR, 2.61; P<.001) and squamous-cell predominance (/ [33.1%] vs / [9.2%]; OR, 4.85; P<.001), markers predicting greater responsiveness.

**Conclusions:** The Black-White esophageal cancer survival gap widened across the contemporary multimodal era. NHB patients were about half as likely to undergo curative-intent surgery, and the dominant measurable driver was the surgical-recommendation step rather than patient refusal. Chemotherapy and radiation receipt showed no comparable disparity, indicating a modality-specific gap rather than generalized undertreatment. Despite favorable tumor biology predicting greater ICI benefit, NHB patients received ICI less often than NHW patients, supporting an access-driven mechanism. Structured multidisciplinary operability review, equitable referral to high-volume surgical centers, and prioritized inclusion of Black patients in ICI trials are the highest-leverage modifiable targets for narrowing the population-level survival gap.
