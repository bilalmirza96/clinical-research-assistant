Note the OBJECTIVE / METHODS / RESULTS / CONCLUSIONS section labels (bench-translational venue style, distinct from the surgical-outcomes Introduction-led structure).

**Title:** Metabolic Reprogramming with Glutamine Antagonism Enhances Anti-Tumor Responses in Colorectal Peritoneal Metastasis

**Objective:** This study investigates the immune landscape and metabolic alterations within the tumor microenvironment of colorectal peritoneal metastasis (CPM). We evaluated the therapeutic potential of glutamine antagonism using Sirpiglenastat (DRP104) in an in vivo CPM model.

**Methods:** Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) was performed on 25 tumor samples from 19 patients with metastatic colorectal cancer (mCRC). Metabolic activity was quantified, and survival analysis was conducted using The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) colorectal cancer (CRC) dataset. An in vivo CPM model was established and treated with DRP104 and anti-PD1 immune checkpoint inhibitors. Treatment response was assessed using IVIS® optical imaging and tumor weight measurements.

**Results:** mCRC samples were classified into three groups: primary tumors, CPM, and liver metastases (LM). CPM exhibited significantly upregulated glutamine metabolism (GM) compared to both primary tumors and LM. Survival analysis revealed that high GM and low cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) signatures were associated with worse overall survival (p<0.02). In vivo treatment arms included Control, DRP104, anti-PD1, and a combination of DRP104 plus anti-PD1. IVIS® imaging demonstrated a significant reduction in tumor burden after 10 days of treatment in mice receiving DRP104 or DRP104 + anti-PD1. The combination therapy showed the greatest therapeutic effect, significantly reducing tumor burden (p<0.05), with mean tumor weights of 2.74 g in controls versus 0.43 g in combination-treated mice (p<0.01).

**Conclusions:** scRNA-seq analysis indicates that tumor cells and tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) in CPM rely heavily on glutamine metabolism. Metabolic reprogramming with glutamine antagonism, particularly in combination with immune checkpoint blockade, may represent a promising therapeutic strategy for colorectal peritoneal metastasis.
